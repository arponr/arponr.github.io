Motivating (co)homology in topology

15 October 2016
$$ \newcommand{ \Ab }{ \operatorname{Ab} } \newcommand{ \AbGroups }{ \mathrm{AbGroups} } \newcommand{ \fin }{ \mathrm{fin} } \newcommand{ \Ho }{ \operatorname{Ho} } \newcommand{ \pt }{ \star } \newcommand{ \Push }{ \operatorname{Push} } \newcommand{ \Sing }{ \operatorname{Sing} } \newcommand{ \Spaces }{ \mathrm{Spaces} } $$

1

Notation.

I currently denote the $\infty$-category of spaces by $\Spaces$ and the one-point space by $\pt \in \Spaces$.

2

Spiel.

The central goal of algebraic topology is to learn about spaces1 by constructing and analyzing algebraic invariants of them. Precisely, an “algebraic invariant” should be a functor $\Spaces \to \sA$, or $\Spaces^\op \to \sA$, where $\sA$ is some kind of “algebraic” category we can get our hands on, e.g. the categories of groups or rings, or perhaps sheaves of such on some geometric object. For this to be fruitful, we’d like to find invariants that both:

  • contain interesting information;
  • are readily computable.

These desiderata are of course in tension with each other. For example, the homotopy group functors $X \goesto \pi_*(X)$ in a sense encode all information in spaces, but are incredibly difficult to compute. Nevertheless, various invariants that are simultaneously interesting and computable have been found; they’re what make algebraic topology worthwhile!

Homology and cohomology theories give one class of (often) interesting but (sometimes) computable invariants. In fact, we will introduce them here from an axiomatic perspective, due to Eilenberg and Steenrod, that essentially says: (co)homology theories are invariants satisfying some axioms ensuring they are somewhat computable.

2.1

Motivation.

The main axiom we will impose is a kind of locality. Let’s discuss this. Suppose we have an invariant of spaces $E \c \Spaces \to \sA$, and we want to compute $E(X)$ for some space $X$. Let’s take the target category $\sA$ of $E$ to be any abelian (discrete) category.2

It’s often the case that we can break $X$ up into smaller pieces $\{U_i\}$ that are easier to understand; e.g., if $X$ is the underlying space of a manifold, then $X$ can be covered by pieces homeomorphic to $\bR^n$, hence homotopy equivalent to a point. It would be nice if $E$ was of a “local” nature, so that knowing the pieces $E(U_i)$ would allow us to compute $E(X)$. Of course, the word “compute” might mean anything, and indeed the form it takes in the definition of (co)homology theories probably isn’t the first thing you’d think of. To partially justify this, let’s see why the thing you actually might think of first wouldn’t make for a great definition.

The categorical/homotopical way to speak about being broken into pieces is via the notion of pushout. Namely, we think of a space $X$ as being the “gluing” of two spaces $U_1$ and $U_2$ along a third space $U_0$ when we are given a pushout square $$ \begin{xy} \xymatrix{ U_0 \ar[r]^{i_1} \ar[d]_{i_2} & U_1 \ar[d]^{j_1} \\ U_2 \ar[r]^{j_2} & X } \end{xy} $$ in the $\infty$-category $\Spaces$. Such a square determines a sequence of maps $$ E(U_0) \lblto{(E(i_1),-E(i_2))} E(U_1) \oplus E(U_2) \lblto{E(j_1) + E(j_2)} E(X); $$ and since $j_1i_1 \iso j_2i_2 \implies E(i_1)E(j_1) = E(i_2)E(j_2)$, we see that the composite of these maps is zero. If we’d like to recover $E(X)$ from $E(U_1)$, $E(U_2)$, and $E(U_0)$, we might first think to ask that this sequence be a short-exact one.

However, I claim that any invariant satisfying this strong locality property (and valued in an abelian category) isn’t too interesting. Suppose given any nonempty space $Y$, suppose we take $U_0 = Y$ and $U_1 = \pt = U_2$, so that $X \iso \Sigma Y$, and suppose we get a short-exact sequence $$ 0 \to E(Y) \lblto{\alpha} E(\pt) \oplus E(\pt) \lblto{\beta} E(X) \to 0 $$ from the above. Now, the maps $i_1,i_2$ both must be the unique map $p \c Y \to \pt$, so $\alpha$ factors as the composite $$ E(Y) \lblto{E(p)} E(\pt) \lblto{\delta} E(\pt) \oplus E(\pt), $$ where $\delta$ denotes the diagonal map. Since $\alpha$ is injective, so must be $E(p)$. In addition, since $Y$ is nonempty, we may choose a map $k \c \pt \to Y$, and then $pk \iso \id_\pt \implies E(p)E(k) = \id_{E(\pt)}$, so $E(p)$ must also be surjective, hence an isomorphism. Thus $E$ assigns the same value $E(\pt)$ to any nonempty space $Y$; hopefully we can agree this won’t be a very interesting invariant.

Observe, though, that it was really the requirement of left-exactness that trivialized the situation above. So what if we just demand that the sequence be right-exact, or just exact in the middle? It turns out that these are indeed much more reasonable demands, and the world is rife with interesting examples. However, once we relax to this situation, it’s natural to demand something else. Namely, we’d like to systematically understand the “failure” of the sequence to be short-exact; one precise way to achieve this would be to extend the sequence on one or both sides, i.e. to put in the context of a long(er) exact sequence. This finally motivates the following definitions of homology and cohomology theories.

3

Let $\sC$ be a $\infty$-category admitting finite colimits (the primary example of interest is $\sC = \Spaces$, but allowing for some variations here—e.g. $\sC = \Spaces^\fin$, the $\infty$-category of finite spaces—will be useful).

3.1

Notation.

Let $\Push(\sC)$ denote the $\infty$-category of pushout squares in $\sC$ (i.e. take the diagram category of squares in $\sC$, and then restrict to the full subcategory of pushout squares).

Let $\rI \c \Push(\sC) \to \sC$ denote the functor taking a pushout square to its initial object (i.e. the object we “glue along”), and $\rF \c \Push(\sC) \to \sC$ the functor taking a pushout square to its final object (i.e. the actual pushout, or “glued” object).

3.2

Notation.

We denote the category of abelian groups by $\AbGroups$.

3.3

Definition.

A homology theory on $\sC$ is a sequence of functors and natural transformations $$ \begin{align*} &E_n \c \sC \to \AbGroups, \\ &\del_n \c E^n \circ \rF \to E^{n-1} \circ \rI \c \Push(\sC) \to \AbGroups \end{align*} $$ for $n \in \bZ$ satisfying the following conditions:

(a)

Additivity: For any set of objects $\{X_i\}$ that admits a coproduct in $\sC$, the canonical map $\bigoplus_i E_n(X_i) \to E_n(\coprod_i X_i)$ is an isomorphism.

(b)

Locality: For any pushout square $\sigma$: $$ \begin{xy} \xymatrix{ U_0 \ar[r]^{i_1} \ar[d]_{i_2} & U_1 \ar[d]^{j_1} \\ U_2 \ar[r]^{j_2} & X } \end{xy} $$ in $\sC$, the sequence $$ \cdots \to E_{n+1}(X) \lblto{\del_{n+1}} E_n(U_0) \lblto{\alpha_n} E_n(U_1) \oplus E(U_2) \lblto{\beta_n} E_n(X) \lblto{\del_n} E_{n-1}(U_0) \to \cdots $$ is exact, where here $$ \alpha_n \ce (E_n(i_1),-E_n(i_2)), \quad \beta_n \ce E_n(j_1) + E_n(j_2). $$

3.4

Definition.

A cohomology theory on $\sC$ is a sequence of functors and natural transformations $$ \begin{align*} &E^n \c \sC^\op \to \AbGroups, \\ &\del^n \c E^n \circ \rI \to E^{n+1} \circ \rF \c \Push(\sC) \to \AbGroups \end{align*} $$ for $n \in \bZ$ satisfying the following conditions:

(a)

Additivity: For any set of objects $\{X_i\}$ that admits a coproduct in $\sC$, the canonical map $E^n(\coprod_i X_i) \to \prod_i E^n(X_i)$ is an isomorphism.

(b)

Locality: For any pushout square $\sigma$: $$ \begin{xy} \xymatrix{ U_0 \ar[r]^{i_1} \ar[d]_{i_2} & U_1 \ar[d]^{j_1} \\ U_2 \ar[r]^{j_2} & X } \end{xy} $$ in $\sC$, the sequence $$ \cdots \to E^{n-1}(U_0) \lblto{\del^{n-1}} E^n(X) \lblto{\alpha^n} E^n(U_1) \oplus E(U_2) \lblto{\beta^n} E^n(U_0) \lblto{\del^n} E^{n+1}(X) \to \cdots $$ is exact, where here $$ \alpha^n \ce (E^n(j_1),-E^n(j_2)), \quad \beta^n \ce E^n(i_1) + E^n(i_2). $$

3.5

Remark.

Note that the functors $E_n$ and $E^n$ in (3.3) and (3.4) automatically factor through the homotopy category $\Ho(\sC)$, as $\AbGroups$ is a discrete category.

3.6

Remark.

The fact that we’ve considered $\lZ$-indexed collections of functors corresponds to the fact that we’re formalizing the situation from our motivating discussion (2.1) where each individual functor is only exact in the middle. If we wanted to instead formalize the situation where we begin with a right-exact functor and then extend it only to the left, we could restrict the above definitions to the case where $E_n$ or $E^n$ vanishes for $n < 0$.

3.7

Warning.

Note that our additivity axioms (3.3a) and (3.4a) are slightly nonstandard, in that they are stated for any set of objects admitting a coproduct in $\sC$, so that the nature of the axiom depends on $\sC$. In particular, though we only assumed finite colimits to make the above definition, if $\sC$ is in fact cocomplete we require infinite additivity.

3.8

Remark.

The locality axioms (3.3b) and (3.4b) are usually named after Mayer and Vietoris, and the exact sequences there are referred to as Mayer-Vietoris sequences.

4

Example.

Our first examples of homology and cohomology theories, on $\sC = \Spaces$, are singular homology and cohomology with coefficients in an abelian group $A$. The underlying functors are denoted on objects by $X \mapsto \rH_n(X;A)$ and $X \mapsto \rH^n(X;A)$, and are uniquely characterized by fitting into a (co)homology theory together with their values on the spheres $\rS^k$ for $k \ge 0$: $$ \rH_n(\rS^k;A) \iso \rH^n(\rS^k;A) \iso \begin{cases} A, & n \in \{0,k\} \\ 0, & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases} $$ I don’t really want to explain the direct constructions of singular homology and cohomology at the moment, but when I get to discussing spectra we’ll see an indirect construction.
