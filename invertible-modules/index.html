Invertible modules

2014, August 19

$$ \newcommand{ \c }{ \colon } \newcommand{ \ce }{ \mathrel{:=} } \newcommand{ \id }{ \mathrm{id} } \newcommand{ \Spec }{ \opname{Spec} } \newcommand{ \Pic }{ \opname{Pic} } \newcommand{ \p }{ \mathfrak{p} } \newcommand{ \m }{ \mathfrak{m} } $$

1.

Notation.

Let $A$ be a commutative ring.

2.

Lemma.

Let $M$ be a finitely generated $A$-module. If $M_\p \iso$ $0$ for some $\p \in \Spec(A)$, then $M_f \iso$ $0$ for some $f \in A - \p$.

2.1.

Proof.

Choose generators $x_1,\ldots,x_n \in M$. If $M_\p \iso$ $0$ then there are $f_1,\ldots,f_n \in A - \p$ such that $f_ix_i = 0$ for $1 \le i \le n$. Taking $f \ce \prod_{i=1}^n f_i \in A - \p$, we have $fM \iso$ $0 \implies M_f \iso$ $0$.

3.

Lemma.

Let $M$ be a finitely generated $A$-module. Then $M$ is locally free of finite rank if and only if $M_\p$ is a finite free $A_\p$-module for each $\p \in \Spec(A)$.

3.1.

Proof.

The only if direction is obvious, so assume $M_\p$ is a finite free $A_\p$-module for each $\p \in \Spec(A)$. Fixing $\p \in \Spec(A)$, it suffices to show there exists $f \in A - \p$ such that $M_f$ is a free $A_f$-module. We have an isomorphism $A_\p^n \iso$ $M_\p$, which without loss of generality is the localisation of a morphism $\alpha \c A^n \to$ $M$. It follows from (2) that since $\alpha_\p$ is an isomorphim, so is $\alpha_f$ for some $f \in A - \p$.

4.

Definition.

We say an $A$-module $M$ is invertible if there exists an $A$-module $N$ and an isomorphism $M \otimes_A N \iso$ $A$.

5.

Lemma.

An invertible $A$-module $M$ is finitely generated.

5.1.

Proof.

Assume we have an $A$-module $N$ and an isomorphism $\alpha \c A \iso$ $M \otimes_A N$. Observe the image of $\alpha$ must be generated by a single element of $M \otimes_A N$, so $\alpha$ must factor through an isomorphism $\beta \c A \iso$ $M_0 \otimes_A N$ for some finitely generated submodule $M_0 \subseteq M$. Then we have an isomorphism

$$ \begin{xy} \xymatrix{ M \ar[d]^{\sim} \ar[r]^{\sim} & M_0 \ar[d]_{\sim} \\ M \otimes_A A \ar[d]^{\sim}_{\id \otimes \beta} & A \otimes_A M_0 \ar[d]_{\sim}^{\alpha \otimes \id} \\ M \otimes_A M_0 \otimes_A N \ar[r]^{\sim} & M \otimes_A N \otimes_A M_0, } \end{xy} $$

implying $M$ is finitely generated.

6.

Lemma.

An $A$-module $M$ is invertible if and only if $M$ is locally free of rank $1$.

6.1.

Proof.

First assume $M$ is invertible: there is an $A$-module $N$ such that $M \otimes_A N \iso$ $A$. Fix any $\p \in \Spec(A)$. By (3) it suffices to show $M_\p$ is free of rank $1$ over $A_\p$. We have

$$ M_\p \otimes_{A_\p} N_\p \iso (M \otimes N)_\p \iso A_\p, $$

i.e. $M_\p$ is invertible. Thus we are reduced to the case that $A$ is a local ring. Let $\m \in \Spec(A)$ denote the maximal ideal and $k \ce A/\m$ the residue field. We have

$$ (M \otimes_A k) \otimes_k (N \otimes_A k) \iso M \otimes_A N \otimes_A k \iso k, $$

i.e. $M \otimes_A k$ is an invertible $k$-module. But $k$ is a field so obviously this implies $M/\m M \iso$ $M \otimes_A k \iso$ $k$. Let $x \in M$ be a lift of a generator of $M/\m M$ and $\alpha \c A \to$ $M$ the map defined by $1 \mapsto x$. By construction the induced map $\alpha \otimes k \c k \to$ $M \otimes_A k$ is an isomorphism. Then Nakayama’s lemma implies $\alpha$ is an isomorphism.

Conversely, assume $M$ is locally free of rank $1$. Let $M^\vee \ce \hom_A(M, A)$ denote the dual of $M$ and consider the canonical evaluation map $M \otimes_A M^\vee \to$ $A$. By hypothesis this morphism locally looks like evaluation $A \otimes_A A^\vee \to$ $A$, which is clearly an isomorphism.1 This finishes the proof.

7.

Definition.

It follows from (5) that the collection $\Pic(A)$ of isomorphism classes of invertible modules forms a set. Under tensor product $\Pic(A)$ forms an abelian group called the Picard group of $A$. Note that by the proof of (6) the inverse in $\Pic(A)$ of (the isomorphism class) of an invertible module $L$ is given by (the isomorphism class of) the dual $L^\vee$.

8.

Lemma.

Let $A_*$ be a graded ring. Let $\otimes$ denote $\otimes_{A_0}$. Assume there is an isomorphism of $A_0$-modules $A_1 \otimes A_{-1} \iso$ $A_0$. Then for $n \ge 1$ we have

$$ A_n \iso A_1^{\otimes n} \quad\text{and}\quad A_{-n} \iso A_{-1}^{\otimes n} \iso (A_1^\vee)^{\otimes n}. $$

8.1.

Proof.

We prove the first isomorphism by induction; the second is symmetric. The case $n=1$ is tautological. Let $n > 1$. Multiplication gives a map $A_1^{\otimes n} \to$ $A_n$. An inverse map is given by

$$ A_n \iso A_n \otimes A_0 \iso A_n \otimes A_{-1} \otimes A_1 \to A_{n-1} \otimes A_1 \iso A_1^{\otimes n}, $$

the last isomorphism coming from the inductive hypothesis.
